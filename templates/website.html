What is the Air Quality Index?

The AQI is an index for reporting daily air quality. It tells you how clean or polluted your air is, and what associated health effects might be a concern for you. The AQI focuses on health effects you may experience within a few hours or days after breathing polluted air. EPA calculates the AQI for five major air pollutants regulated by the Clean Air Act: ground-level ozone, particle pollution (also known as particulate matter), carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, and nitrogen dioxide. For each of these pollutants, EPA has established national air quality standards to protect public health .Ground-level ozone and airborne particles are the two pollutants that pose the greatest threat to human health in this country.

(Source: AirNow.gov)

How about Ozone?

Ozone, a gas composed of three atoms of oxygen (O3), occurs both in the Earth's upper atmosphere and at ground level. Ozone can be good or bad, depending on where it is found. “Good” ozone (also called 'stratospheric ozone') occurs naturally in the upper atmosphere, where it forms a protective layer that shields us from the sun's harmful ultraviolet rays. This beneficial ozone has been partially destroyed by man-made chemicals. “Bad” ozone is found at ground level and is a harmful air pollutant, because of its health effects on people and the environment, and it is the main ingredient in smog.
Evidence from observational studies strongly indicates that higher daily ozone concentrations are associated with increased asthma attacks, increased hospital admissions, increased daily mortality, and other markers of morbidity.

(Source: EPA.gov)



Different State, Different Rates

The natural concentration of ozone is about 0.01 parts per million (or 10 parts per billion). According to the Environmental Protection Agency, exposure to ozone levels greater than 0.07 parts per million (or 70 parts per billion) for 8 hours is unhealthy. The highest levels of ozone from years 2013-2018 were found mainly in the southwest region of the US, with peak ozone levels measured at 0.062 parts per million (or 62 parts per billion) in Clear Creek County, Colorado. The lowest levels of ozone were found in Juncos, Puerto Rico at 0.006 parts per million (or 6 parts per billion).
